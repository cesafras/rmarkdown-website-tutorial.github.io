Millennial version / 2010-2017

Read this if you like: QUEENIE by Candice Carty-Williams, NORMAL PEOPLE by Sally Rooney, INDUSTRY on HBO, love stories, coming-of-age stories, flawed female characters, flawed male characters, workplace novels, arguments in taxis, awkward sex scenes, when it's funny, but not ha-ha funny.

In 2010, Jess is a senior at an Ivy League college, making one careless decision after another. Drinking too much. Dating the wrong guys. Osmosing the values of her privileged friends. Yet even as Jess craves acceptance and belonging, she also feels that her classmates are, in her words: “all racist dicks.” Especially preppy know-it-all Josh, who isn’t shy to broadcast his infuriating and insensitive opinions.

But when Jess and Josh end up working at the same investment bank after graduation, an unlikely friendship forms. Eventually they fall in love. They are different—he is white, she is black; he is conservative, she is liberal; he is sure of his place in the world, she is just beginning to discover hers—but the momentum of being young and in love propels their relationship forward despite their divergent worldviews. That is, until the 2016 election. When the cultural landscape shifts underneath them, Jess is forced to finally ask whether they are meant to be together and whether, in fact, everything’s fine.

CHAPTER 1

Jess’s first day of work, the first day of the rest of her life. Into the elevator and up to the twentieth floor, where the doors open with a little whoosh. The entire building smells like money.

She receives a small plaque with her name printed in formal looking letters: JESSICA JONES, INVESTMENT BANKING ANALYST. Then the introductions—the other analysts on the team: Brad and John and Rich and Tom, or maybe it’s Rich and Tom and Brad and John—and there is also Josh, who Jess remembers from college.

“Hey,” she says, “it’s you!”

He looks up from his desk—he is already installed at a workstation, looking busy and important—but his face is blank.

“Remember me?” she asks. They had a class together last year and Jess remembers him, because he was the worst.

“Jess?” she offers, “From school?”

He blinks.

"We had a class together?” she tries, “Law & Society?”

He is just looking at her, saying nothing. Is it possible she has something on her face?

“With Kaplan? Fall seme—”

“I remember you,” he finally says. And then promptly swivels in his chair.

Cool, Jess thinks, Nice catching up.

She starts to go.

“You know,” he says, not turning, “I knew you’d been assigned to this desk.”

Jess stops, “Oh really?”

He nods—the back of his head—“I worked with these guys when I was here last summer. And I started off-cycle, so I’ve been back since January.” He pauses. “They asked me about you.”

“What did you say?”

“Nothing.”

"What! Why didn’t you tell them I was amazing?”

“Because,” he says, finally turning to look at her, “I’m not convinced you are amazing.”

CHAPTER 1

Jo’s first day of work, the first day of the rest of her life. Into the elevator and up to the twentieth floor, where the doors open with a little whoosh. The entire building smells like money.

She receives a small plaque with her name printed in formal looking letters: JOSEPHINE JONES, MERGERS & ACQUISITIONS. Then the introductions—the other analysts on the team: Don and Robert and Gary and Tom, or maybe it’s Robert and Gary and Tom and Don—and there is also John Barrett, who Jo remembers from college.

“Hey,” she says, “it’s you!”

He looks up from his desk—he is already installed at a workstation, looking busy and important—but his face is blank.

“Remember me?” she asks.

They were in college together, same major, same year.

“Josephine? Jones?” she offers, “From school?”

He blinks.

“We had a class together?” she tries, “Fall term?”

He is just looking at her, saying nothing. If he truly doesn’t remember her, it would be an extraordinary act of forgetting—there was only one other black girl in their entire class.

“Tuesdays and Thursdays?” she prods, “In Dietrich Hall? Remember—”

“I remember you,” he cuts her off. And then promptly swivels in his chair.

Wonderful, Jo thinks, Nice catching up.

She starts to go.

“You know,” he says, not turning, “I knew you’d been assigned to this desk.”

Jo stops, “Oh really?”

He nods—the back of his head—“I worked with these guys when I was here last summer. And I started off-cycle, so I’ve been back since January.” He pauses. “They asked me about you.”

“What did you say?”

“Nothing.”

“What! Why didn’t you tell them I was amazing?”

“Because,” he says, finally turning to look at her, “I’m not convinced you are amazing.”
